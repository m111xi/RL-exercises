NOTE: We used LLMs to help us Code Level 3, almost all code was AI generated. But we still came up with the contectual features and the general concept.

We defined train/test/(optional) validation context sets like in Sec. 6.4: interpolation if at least one context feature lies within the train marginal support, extrapolation if both features are outside. Here the training set was joints [0, 3] (friction indices {0,1} and horizon index {0}).

Comparing blind (one policy trained on the mixture MDP of the train contexts) vs oracle (optimal policy computed per context), we see:

On many interpolation contexts the gap is small to moderate (e.g., joints 3/4/5: ~5–13).
There is a large failure case even within interpolation: joint 6 is labeled interpolation, but blind ≈ 8 vs oracle ≈ 85 (gap ≈ 77). So “interpolation” by the marginal-overlap definition does not guarantee good performance when the other feature shifts strongly.
On extrapolation contexts (joints 7/8) the gap is largest (≈ 42 and ≈ 127), consistent with the idea that performance drops most outside the training distribution.
Negative gaps (oracle < blind) on a few contexts are likely due to rollout noise / limited evaluation episodes rather than the oracle being truly worse.
